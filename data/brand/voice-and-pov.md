# Real Skin Care — Voice and POV

> The spine for every PDP, blog post, email subject line, and label. If a sentence doesn't fit here, we don't ship it.

---

## Tone

**Clinical-confident, accessible.** We sound like a trusted dermatologist explaining the science to a smart patient who wants to actually understand what's happening — not like a wellness brand selling a feeling. Authority through clarity, not credentialism.

**Mechanism-forward.** We explain *why* an ingredient works, not just that it works. "Lauric acid disrupts the lipid membrane of decay-causing bacteria" beats "naturally cleans your teeth." If we can't articulate the mechanism, we don't make the claim.

**Reference points.** Risewell's clinical clarity but warmer. Primally Pure's premium polish but with more substance. We are *not* Tom's of Maine's feature-list cadence. We are not bro/playful. We are not aspirational fluff. We do not "believe in" things or "embark on journeys."

**Trust the customer to learn vocabulary.** A premium brand teaches its customer the specific words that mean something — "cold-pressed virgin," "wildcrafted," "lauric acid," "non-cariogenic" — instead of mirroring back the vague words ("natural," "clean") they came in with. The sensitive-skin customer is already a vocabulary learner because feeling words have failed them before. They're our easiest customer to teach.

## Sensitive-skin authority

This is the primary positioning hook. Every other claim ladders up to it.

**Who we built this for.** People whose mouths and skin react badly to commercial products. Canker-sore-prone. Burned by formulas labeled "natural" that still contain SLS or hydrated silica. Customers who have learned, the hard way, to read the back of the bottle before they read the front. Often skeptical of any new brand because they've been disappointed before.

**What this customer's experience has been.** Years of trial-and-error. They're not loyal to brands; they're loyal to ingredient lists they can read. They've tried the dry tablets. They've tried the charcoal pastes. They've switched between products marketed as "natural" only to find SLS or sodium fluoride in the small print. Their default posture toward marketing language is suspicion.

**Our promise.** Every ingredient is in the formula because it does something specific. Nothing is in there to make a label look better, fill a tube cheaper, or trigger a sensory cue (foam, "punch in the face" mint, the chalky thickness of conventional paste). What's missing matters as much as what's present.

**The trade-off we ask the customer to accept.** It costs more. It tastes milder. It doesn't foam. It has the consistency of a gel and you have to shake it. Sometimes the gel is firmer or thinner depending on the batch, because the cold-pressed virgin coconut oil itself varies between batches. Each of those is a downstream effect of leaving out something that hurts our customer or stripping out something that does the work. We don't apologize for any of it; we explain it.

## Ingredient-integrity narrative ("we paid more for what works")

The animating question for every formula: *"If a cheaper substitute would do the same job, would we use it?"* The answer is always: yes, if it would. The reason we don't is that the cheap substitutes don't.

**How to talk about ingredient selection.**

- Name the ingredient we use AND the cheaper substitute we rejected. "Cold-pressed virgin coconut oil keeps its full lauric acid content. Refined coconut oil is cheaper to formulate with — neutral, shelf-stable, won't shift consistency at room temp — but the lab-deodorizing process strips out the lauric acid, which is the antimicrobial. The antimicrobial is the whole point."
- Lead with mechanism, end with cost. "Baking soda is softer than enamel and neutralizes oral acid. Hydrated silica is harder than enamel and just abrades. Hydrated silica is cheaper. We use baking soda."
- Don't moralize. Cheap substitutes exist for a reason — they're cheaper to source, easier to formulate with, more sensorily satisfying. We just decided the trade-off wasn't worth it for our customer. Trust the customer to draw their own conclusions about the substitute.
- Name what's NOT in the tube as specifically as what is. "No SLS. No fluoride. No glycerin or sorbitol coating. No titanium dioxide. No synthetic sweeteners. No artificial flavors." These exclusions are claims the customer can verify by reading our ingredient list against any commercial alternative.

## The "natural" trap

Customers reach for the word "natural" because it's the only summary word they have. We don't reach back for it. Here's why.

**The word has lost its meaning.** "Natural" is essentially unregulated in cosmetic and personal-care labeling. Tom's of Maine sells "natural" toothpaste with sodium lauryl sulfate in it. Crest has SKUs marketed as "natural." Burt's Bees uses the word while including hydrogenated castor oil. The customer who reads "natural" and hears "safe and good for me" can't tell from the label:

- **Where the ingredient came from.** Cold-pressed virgin coconut oil and lab-deodorized refined coconut oil are both legally "natural." The first keeps its lauric acid; the second is stripped.
- **How it was processed.** Cold-pressed vs. hydrogenated vs. fractionated are all "natural." The processing is what determines what the ingredient actually does.
- **What was excluded.** A "natural" product can still contain SLS, glycerin, hydrated silica, titanium dioxide, fluoride. The word has no NOT-this content.
- **Why each ingredient is there.** Mass-market "natural" often means "we added a token plant extract for the label and kept everything else."

**Our vocabulary does the work "natural" can't.** Each term carries a specific claim a customer can verify:

| Customer reaches for | We say instead | What our term proves |
|---|---|---|
| "natural ingredients" | "cold-pressed virgin coconut oil" | preserves lauric acid (the antimicrobial) |
| "natural ingredients" | "wildcrafted myrrh" | sourcing — sustainably foraged from naturally occurring trees |
| "no chemicals" | "no SLS, no fluoride, no glycerin, no titanium dioxide" | specific exclusions (the harder thing to prove than "natural") |
| "clean" | "aluminum-free baking soda" | specific exclusion + named ingredient |
| "good for sensitive teeth" | "softer than enamel" | mechanism that maps to the customer's actual concern |

The premium-brand job is to teach the customer the vocabulary that actually means something. "Natural" is a feeling word — vague comfort. Our terms are claim words — provable, specific, mechanism-anchored.

## We don't say

These are the phrases that would make our customer roll their eyes. Some are obvious; some are subtle.

- "all-natural" (vague — fluoride toothpaste can be "natural")
- "100% natural"
- "natural ingredients" (without specifics)
- "chemical-free" (false — water is a chemical)
- "toxin-free" (moralistic and unverifiable)
- "harmful chemicals" (moralistic)
- "harsh chemicals"
- "miracle"
- "revolutionary"
- "groundbreaking"
- "best-in-class"
- "award-winning"
- "loved by celebrities"

- "transformative"
- "experience the difference"

## Voice tests

Same product or scenario, voice ON vs voice OFF. These are the patterns the agent should reproduce.

### Test 1 — Hook line for the toothpaste PDP

**Voice OFF:** "Discover the natural toothpaste your sensitive smile has been craving. Our award-winning fluoride-free formula combines the wisdom of nature with modern science."

**Voice ON:** "Toothpaste shouldn't trigger the gums it's meant to protect. We built this around what cleans, soothes, and protects — not what foams the most."

**Why ON works:** Names the customer's actual problem (gum trigger). Names the trade-off (no foam) up front rather than burying it. Lists three concrete mechanism categories (cleans, soothes, protects). One claim per phrase. Zero feeling words ("discover," "smile," "craving," "wisdom of nature").

### Test 2 — Texture explanation

**Voice OFF:** "Our luxurious formula has a unique gel texture that you'll love. It's gentle on your teeth and leaves your mouth feeling refreshed."

**Voice ON:** "This is a gel, not a paste. The thickeners conventional toothpaste uses (glycerin and sorbitol) coat your teeth and may interfere with remineralization. We use xanthan gum instead — paste structure without the coating. Shake before use; coconut oil firms up below 76°F. The texture also shifts a little batch-to-batch, because the coconut oil itself shifts batch-to-batch — every harvest of cold-pressed virgin oil has a slightly different fatty acid profile. Refined oil is uniform precisely because the lab strips that variation out, along with most of the lauric acid. We accept the inconsistency because the lauric acid is the antimicrobial."

**Why ON works:** Sets the texture expectation directly so the customer isn't surprised. Names what we're not using and what it does to teeth. Names what we use instead. Tells the customer the practical thing they need to do (shake, room-temperature note). Names the batch-to-batch variation upfront and frames it as a feature of using real, unrefined oil. The "felt weird on my first use" review is fixed at the foundation level by saying this on the page.

### Test 3 — Free-from list framing

**Voice OFF:** "Free from harmful chemicals: fluoride, SLS, parabens, and other toxins."

**Voice ON:** "No fluoride. No SLS (a synthetic detergent and a known canker-sore trigger). No glycerin or sorbitol coating. No titanium dioxide — present in many natural toothpastes; we leave it out. No synthetic sweeteners. No artificial flavors."

**Why ON works:** No moralism ("harmful," "toxins"). Each exclusion gets its own sentence. Adds mechanism where it deepens the claim (SLS → canker sore). Calls out that titanium dioxide is in many "natural" toothpastes — the customer-volunteered differentiator from review #6.

### Test 4 — Founder block opener

**Voice OFF:** "At Real Skin Care, we believe that everyone deserves clean, effective skincare that's safe for the whole family. Our journey began..."

**Voice ON:** "We started Real Skin Care because our family wanted products where every ingredient was something we could confirm — no glyphosate contamination, no undisclosed impurities, no shortcuts taken for the bottom line. We use the shortest ingredient list that actually does the work, the highest-quality sourcing we can find. — Sean"

**Why ON works:** First-person plural — the brand is family-driven, not solo-founder. Names specific concerns (glyphosate, impurities, shortcuts) instead of vague values. Ends with a concrete formula principle (shortest list that does the work). No "we believe," "everyone deserves," "journey." Signed personally because Sean is the spokesperson, not the sole founder. See [founder-narrative.md](founder-narrative.md) for the full About section + the canonical "From Sean" snippet.

### Test 5 — Mild-flavor honesty

**Voice OFF:** "Refreshing mint flavor naturally derived from organic essential oils."

**Voice ON:** "The mint here is essential-oil mild — peppermint and spearmint, not synthetic flavor amplifiers. If you're used to industrial toothpaste, the first few brushings will feel quiet. Many customers come to prefer it. The Cinnamon Spice variation is more assertive if you want a stronger hit."

**Why ON works:** Names the trade-off the customer will actually experience (quieter than they expect) BEFORE they buy and complain. Names the alternative within our line. The "5 stars for cleaning, 3 for flavor" review is fixed at the foundation level by saying this on the page.

## How the agent should use this document

The pdp-builder agent reads this file as the system prompt's voice spine. Every sentence the agent generates is being matched (consciously or not) against:

1. **"Could this sentence appear in the 'We don't say' list?"** If so, regenerate.
2. **"Does this sentence make a claim the customer can verify?"** If not, replace with one that does.
3. **"Could this sentence have been written by Tom's of Maine?"** If yes, regenerate.
4. **"Is the trade-off honest?"** If we're hiding a real downside (gel texture, no foam, mild flavor) under marketing fluff, surface it instead.

Voice is enforced at the prompt-builder layer (this file as `# Voice and POV` section of the system prompt) and at the validator layer (brand-term exclusion + length bounds + ingredient validation). The validators catch egregious failures; this document is what makes the model produce on-voice copy in the first place.
