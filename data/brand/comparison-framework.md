# Comparison Framework

How we describe our formulas against the mass-market alternative without naming brands. Read alongside [voice-and-pov.md](voice-and-pov.md) — same voice rules apply.

---

## Axes we differentiate on

### Toothpaste-relevant (pilot-blocking)

- **SLS (sodium lauryl sulfate).** Mass-market includes SLS as a synthetic detergent that creates the foam customers expect. We exclude it. SLS is a known canker-sore trigger for people prone to them, and foam doesn't clean — your brush does. Foam is a sensory cue trained into the customer over decades of advertising, not a mechanism.

- **Fluoride.** Mass-market includes fluoride as the standard cavity-prevention chemistry. We exclude it because we formulated for customers with sensitive teeth and gums who prefer to avoid it — many of whom can pair our paste with a fluoride rinse if they want both. The cleaning and antimicrobial work in our formula is done by lauric acid (coconut oil), pH balancing (baking soda), and resin sesquiterpenes (myrrh) — independent of fluoride.

- **Hydrated silica.** Mass-market uses hydrated silica as the primary abrasive. It's cheap and creates the gritty paste texture customers expect. Hydrated silica is harder than enamel; over years of use it wears down sensitive teeth. We use baking soda — softer than enamel, gentler abrasion, with the bonus of pH neutralization.

- **Glycerin / sorbitol coating.** Mass-market uses glycerin and sorbitol as cheap thickeners and humectants — they give toothpaste its paste consistency and sweetness. They also leave a film on teeth that may interfere with natural remineralization. We use xanthan gum (a fermentation-derived polysaccharide) for paste structure without the coating. Trade-off: our texture is closer to gel than paste.

- **Titanium dioxide.** Mass-market — including many "natural" brands — uses titanium dioxide as a whitening agent that makes the paste look bright white. The EU has restricted it as a food additive (E171) over cellular toxicity concerns; cosmetic-use safety is still debated. We exclude it. Our paste is not titanium-white — it's the color of its actual ingredients.

- **Synthetic sweeteners.** Mass-market uses sodium saccharin, sucralose, or aspartame because they're cheap, shelf-stable, and intensely sweet. We use organic stevia leaf extract — non-cariogenic, plant-derived, no sugar alcohols. Trade-off: stevia is more bitter on the palate than synthetics, so the paste tastes milder.

- **Synthetic flavors.** Mass-market uses artificial peppermint and spearmint flavors that read as "punch in the face" mint. The amplifier is a chemical, not the plant. We use organic essential oils of peppermint and spearmint — milder, cleaner, closer to the actual plant. The Cinnamon Spice variation is naturally more assertive than the mint variations because cinnamon essential oil is naturally stronger than mint essential oil.

### Other clusters (forward-looking; not pilot-blocking)

- **Aluminum (deodorant).** Mass-market deodorants use aluminum compounds (aluminum chlorohydrate, aluminum zirconium) as antiperspirants — they plug sweat ducts. Customer concerns about long-term aluminum exposure drove much of the natural-deodorant category. We exclude aluminum and rely on antimicrobial coconut oil and baking soda to control odor (not perspiration).

- **Synthetic fragrance.** Mass-market uses "fragrance" or "parfum" — a regulatory umbrella that can hide hundreds of undisclosed compounds, many of which are common skin sensitizers. We use named organic essential oils only, listed individually on the back panel. The customer can read what's making the product smell the way it does.

- **Refined vs. virgin oils.** Mass-market uses refined coconut oil because it's neutral, shelf-stable, and won't shift consistency at room temperature. The refining process (deodorizing, sometimes hydrogenating) strips most of the lauric acid — which is what makes virgin coconut oil antimicrobial. We use cold-pressed virgin oil and accept the texture variation that comes with it.

- **Parabens.** Mass-market uses methylparaben and propylparaben as cheap, broad-spectrum preservatives. Customer concerns over endocrine disruption have driven much of the "paraben-free" movement. We don't use parabens; our shelf-stable formulas use other preservation strategies (essential oils with antimicrobial activity, water activity management).

## Voice rules for comparisons

- **Never name a competitor brand directly in product copy.** Internal reference points (Tom's of Maine, Crest, Burt's Bees, Risewell, Primally Pure) inform our voice and our positioning, but they don't appear on the page.

- **Use "mass-market," "conventional," or "the industry standard" as the contrast term.** Vary which one to avoid repetition.

- **State the trade-off plainly:** "they pay less, you pay more, here's what the difference does."

- **Lean on mechanism, not moralism.** "Hydrated silica is harder than enamel" beats "hydrated silica is bad." "SLS is a known canker-sore trigger" beats "SLS is harmful." Trust the customer to draw their own conclusions from the mechanism.

- **Acknowledge the cost honestly.** Cheap substitutes exist for a reason — they're cheaper to source, easier to formulate with, more sensorily satisfying. We just decided the trade-off wasn't worth it for our customer. Don't pretend the alternatives are bad on every dimension.

- **The "natural" trap rule.** When contrasting against other "natural" brands (not just conventional ones), use the same axes. Many "natural" toothpastes still include hydrated silica, glycerin, titanium dioxide. The differentiation isn't natural-vs-conventional; it's specific-exclusion-vs-marketing-word. See [voice-and-pov.md](voice-and-pov.md) → "The 'natural' trap."

- **Lead the comparison from our customer's experience, not from chemistry textbooks.** "If you've ever had a canker sore the day after switching toothpastes, that's SLS" beats "SLS is a synthetic detergent commonly added to dentifrice formulations." Use the chemistry to back up the customer experience, not to replace it.
