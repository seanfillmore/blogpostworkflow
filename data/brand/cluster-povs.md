## toothpaste

For sensitive teeth, sensitive gums, and customers who've decided to leave fluoride out of their routine, the standard commercial toothpaste isn't built for them — it's built for the foam, the punch-in-the-face mint, and the bright-white paste look that mass-market advertising trained generations to expect. None of those are mechanisms. They're sensory cues. We built this for the customer who already learned that the hard way: people whose mouths react to SLS with canker sores, whose enamel has been worn thin by years of hydrated-silica abrasion, whose gums flare from synthetic flavor amplifiers, whose suspicion of "natural" labeling is well-earned.

The mechanism story is short. Cold-pressed virgin coconut oil delivers lauric acid, which disrupts the lipid membranes of decay-causing bacteria — the same job synthetic antimicrobials do in commercial paste, without the irritation. Baking soda neutralizes the acidic environment cavity-causing bacteria need, and polishes stains with a micro-abrasion gentler than enamel. Wildcrafted myrrh's sesquiterpenes are anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial, particularly soothing for irritated gums — used in oral care for thousands of years. Xanthan gum gives the paste structure without the glycerin or sorbitol coating that may interfere with natural remineralization. Stevia sweetens it (non-cariogenic — doesn't feed cavity-causing bacteria). Organic peppermint, spearmint, or cinnamon essential oils for flavor, depending on variation.

What's NOT in the tube matters as much as what is. No fluoride. No SLS (a known canker-sore trigger). No hydrated silica (harder than enamel; abrades over years). No glycerin or sorbitol coating. No titanium dioxide (present in many "natural" pastes; we leave it out). No synthetic sweeteners. No artificial flavors.

The trade-off is honest. The texture is more gel than paste — closer to a thick lotion than a conventional squeeze-tube paste. It doesn't foam. The mint is essential-oil mild, not synthetic-flavor amplified. Sometimes the gel is firmer or thinner depending on the batch, because cold-pressed virgin coconut oil itself varies between batches — refined oil is uniform precisely because the lab strips that variation out, along with most of the lauric acid. Each part of the trade-off is a downstream effect of leaving something out, or refusing to strip the variation that lets the work happen. Bonus: many customers report teeth feel polished after use — a side effect of the lauric-acid-plus-baking-soda combination doing its work without the residue conventional paste leaves behind.
