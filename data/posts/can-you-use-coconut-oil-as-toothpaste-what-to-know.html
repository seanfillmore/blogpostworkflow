Quick Answer: Can You Use Coconut Oil as Toothpaste?

Yes, you can use coconut oil to brush your teeth. It has real antimicrobial properties, mainly thanks to its lauric acid content, which research shows can inhibit the growth of Streptococcus mutans — one of the key bacteria linked to tooth decay. That makes it a legitimate natural option for supplementing your oral care routine. However, coconut oil does not contain fluoride or any remineralizing agents, and it lacks the mild abrasives found in conventional toothpaste. It should not be treated as a complete, drop-in replacement for a dentist-approved routine — especially if you're cavity-prone. Think of it as a useful addition, not a full substitute.

Why People Use Coconut Oil for Brushing Teeth

Interest in using coconut oil for oral care has grown steadily over the past decade. A lot of it comes down to what people want to avoid — synthetic foaming agents, artificial flavors, and other additives found in many conventional toothpastes. The appeal of a single, recognizable ingredient is real.

There's also a historical angle. Oil pulling — swishing oil in the mouth to draw out bacteria — is a traditional Ayurvedic practice with roots going back thousands of years. Coconut oil became the modern go-to for this because of its taste and its fatty acid profile. As awareness of oil pulling grew, so did interest in using coconut oil for brushing as well.

It's worth being clear: the traditional practice and the modern research are not the same thing. We'll walk through both, so you know exactly what's supported and what's still being studied.

Benefits of Using Coconut Oil on Your Teeth

There are several real, research-informed reasons people reach for coconut oil as part of their oral care routine. Here's what the evidence actually shows.

  • Antimicrobial action: Coconut oil is rich in lauric acid, a medium-chain fatty acid with documented antibacterial properties. A study published in the Journal of Contemporary Dental Practice found that lauric acid effectively inhibited Streptococcus mutans, a primary cavity-causing bacterium. This is the most evidence-backed benefit of coconut oil for oral health.
  • Plaque and gum health: Several studies have examined coconut oil pulling specifically and found measurable reductions in plaque scores and gingivitis markers. It's important to note that most of this research involves oil pulling — swishing for 10–20 minutes — not brushing. The distinction matters when interpreting the evidence.
  • Mild surface whitening: Coconut oil may help remove certain surface stains over time. Its ability to bind to lipid-soluble deposits on tooth enamel can contribute to a brighter appearance. This is gentle surface whitening only — not bleaching. If you want to explore this further, our post on coconut whitening toothpaste goes deeper on how this works.
  • Breath freshening: Because coconut oil's antimicrobial action can reduce the bacteria that produce volatile sulfur compounds, it may help freshen breath. The effect is real, even if it's more modest than what you'd get from a minty toothpaste.
  • No harsh synthetic additives: For people with sensitivities to certain conventional toothpaste ingredients, plain coconut oil offers a clean, simple alternative with nothing artificial to react to.

Limitations: What Coconut Oil Cannot Do for Your Teeth

This part matters just as much as the benefits. Being honest about limitations is how you make a smart choice — and it's what separates credible guidance from wellness hype.

  • No fluoride: Coconut oil does not remineralize enamel the way fluoride does. For people at higher cavity risk, this is a significant gap. If you're considering going fluoride-free, read our guide on fluoride-free toothpaste: what to use and why it works before making the switch. And always talk to your dentist first.
  • No abrasive cleaning action: Conventional toothpastes use mild abrasives to help remove tartar buildup. Plain coconut oil has none of that. It can reduce bacteria, but it won't scrub away calcified deposits on its own.
  • Limited research on brushing specifically: The strongest clinical evidence for coconut oil in oral care relates to oil pulling, not brushing. That distinction matters. Brushing with coconut oil likely delivers some of the same antimicrobial benefits, but the specific evidence base is thinner.
  • Drain issues: Coconut oil solidifies below about 76°F. If you rinse it down the sink repeatedly, it can build up in your pipes. Always spit into a trash bin or paper towel.
  • Not a substitute for professional dental care: No natural product replaces regular cleanings and checkups. If you're using coconut oil as part of your routine, keep seeing your dentist.

Coconut Oil Pulling vs. Brushing With Coconut Oil

These two practices often get lumped together, but they're different. It's worth knowing which one you're actually doing.

Oil pulling means swishing about one tablespoon of coconut oil in your mouth for 10–20 minutes, then spitting it out — into a trash bin, not the sink. This is the practice with the most published research behind it. The extended contact time allows the oil to interact with bacteria throughout the mouth, including between teeth and along the gumline.

Brushing with coconut oil means applying coconut oil to a toothbrush and brushing as you normally would for about two minutes. This is less studied, but it still delivers antimicrobial contact and gives you the mechanical cleaning action that brushing provides.

You can combine both. Some people oil pull first to reduce bacterial load, then brush with coconut oil (or a formulated natural toothpaste) afterward. If you're going to try one, oil pulling has more research support. If brushing is more practical for your routine, it's still a reasonable choice — just calibrate your expectations accordingly.

How to Brush Your Teeth With Coconut Oil (Step-by-Step)

  1. Scoop or squeeze about ½ teaspoon of organic virgin coconut oil. If you're using a jar, use a clean utensil — not your fingers — to avoid contaminating the supply.
  2. If it's solid, let it melt. Place it on your toothbrush and let it soften for a few seconds, or let it melt on your tongue before you start brushing.
  3. Brush all surfaces for 2 minutes. Cover the front, back, and chewing surfaces of every tooth. Work along the gumline too.
  4. Spit into a trash bin or paper towel. Not the sink. Coconut oil solidifies as it cools and can clog pipes over time.
  5. Rinse thoroughly with water. Swish and spit until your mouth feels clean.
  6. Follow with your regular toothpaste if you'd like. There's no rule against combining approaches. Many people brush with coconut oil first and follow up with a fluoride toothpaste or a clean natural formula.

Always use organic virgin coconut oil for oral use. Refined coconut oil has been processed and may have fewer of the naturally occurring beneficial compounds.

DIY Coconut Oil Toothpaste Recipes

If you want to take a hands-on approach, these two recipes are simple to make and use ingredients you can find at most grocery stores.

Recipe 1 — Basic Coconut Oil Toothpaste

  • 2 tablespoons organic virgin coconut oil (softened)
  • 1 tablespoon baking soda
  • 5 drops organic peppermint essential oil

Mix thoroughly until smooth. Store in a small glass jar. Makes about a 2–3 week supply.

Recipe 2 — Gentle Whitening Version

  • 2 tablespoons organic virgin coconut oil (softened)
  • 1 tablespoon baking soda
  • 1 teaspoon food-grade calcium carbonate powder (adds mild abrasive whitening action)
  • 5 drops organic peppermint essential oil

Mix well and store in a glass jar. The calcium carbonate adds a touch more cleaning power for surface stains.

Storage and Safety Tips

  • Store in a small, clean glass jar with a lid.
  • Use within 2–3 weeks.
  • Always scoop with a clean spoon or spatula — not your fingers — to keep the batch uncontaminated.
  • Keep away from water to prevent the mixture from separating.

Can You Use Coconut Oil as Toothpaste for Dogs?

This comes up more than you'd think, and it's a smart question. Coconut oil is generally considered safe for dogs in small amounts — it's not toxic if ingested, which is a meaningful advantage over many human toothpaste ingredients. Xylitol, for example, is a common sweetener in human toothpastes that is seriously dangerous for dogs. Coconut oil has no such concern.

You can apply a small amount of coconut oil to a dog toothbrush or finger brush and gently clean your dog's teeth. It may help reduce bacterial buildup and freshen their breath. Most dogs don't mind the taste, which helps with compliance.

That said, coconut oil is not a substitute for veterinary-formulated dog toothpaste. Dog-specific formulas are designed with the right enzymes and safety profiles for animals. And while brushing at home helps, it doesn't replace a professional veterinary dental cleaning.

Always check with your vet before changing your pet's oral care routine — especially if your dog has any existing dental or health conditions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I brush my teeth with coconut oil only?
You can — at least in the short term. Coconut oil has real antimicrobial benefits and will clean your teeth to a degree. But as a long-term replacement for toothpaste, it has gaps: no abrasive cleaning action and no remineralizing support. If you want to go fluoride-free long-term, a formulated natural toothpaste that includes baking soda and botanical actives will serve you better. Talk to your dentist before making any permanent changes, especially if you're cavity-prone.

How often should I use coconut oil for oral care?
Daily use as a supplement to your regular routine is fine for most people. If you're using it as your primary toothpaste, that's a bigger commitment — and one worth discussing with your dentist first. As a starting point, many people brush with coconut oil in the morning and use their regular toothpaste at night.

Does coconut oil prevent cavities?
Some research shows coconut oil reduces the bacteria most associated with cavities — specifically Streptococcus mutans. But there's no conclusive evidence that coconut oil prevents cavities the way fluoride does. Reducing harmful bacteria is a meaningful benefit, but it's not the same as proven cavity prevention. Don't use this as a reason to skip dental checkups.

Is coconut oil toothpaste safe for kids?
Coconut oil itself is safe for children. But kids' oral care needs are different from adults' — especially around cavity prevention and developing enamel. Always check with a pediatric dentist before switching a child's toothpaste to any fluoride-free formula, whether homemade or commercial.

Can you use coconut oil as toothpaste for dogs?
Yes, coconut oil is generally considered safe for dogs and is not toxic if swallowed — unlike xylitol, which is found in many human toothpastes and is dangerous for pets. It can help with bacterial buildup and breath. That said, see the dedicated section above for full details, and always check with your vet before changing your dog's dental routine.
