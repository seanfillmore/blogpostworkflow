What's New: The PFAS Wake-Up Call

The biggest skincare news of late 2025 changed the conversation around "non-toxic" forever. In December 2025, the FDA released its first comprehensive report on PFAS in cosmetics, identifying 51 different PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, known as "forever chemicals") intentionally added to 1,744 cosmetic products sold in the United States.

While PFAS appeared in less than 0.5% of all cosmetics, the report flagged one specific PFAS — perfluorohexylethyl triethoxysilane — as a potential safety concern when used in body lotion at high concentrations. PFAS are linked to immune system suppression, increased cancer risk, and environmental persistence, meaning they don't break down naturally and accumulate in our bodies and waterways.

Eleven states have already passed legislation banning intentionally added PFAS from cosmetics between 2025 and 2028, and major brands announced 100% PFAS-free formulas by the end of 2024. The shift toward truly clean body care has never been more urgent — or more possible.

Ingredients to Avoid in Body Lotion

  • PFAS (Forever Chemicals): Look for "fluoro," "perfluoro," or "PTFE" in the ingredient name. These chemicals persist in the environment and accumulate in the body, with potential links to immune suppression and cancer.
  • Synthetic Fragrance: Can hide dozens of undisclosed chemicals, including phthalates. The term "fragrance" on a label can represent any combination of 3,000+ ingredients, many never tested for safety.
  • Parabens (methylparaben, propylparaben, butylparaben): Preservatives linked to hormone disruption.
  • PEGs and Polysorbates: Can be contaminated with 1,4-dioxane and ethylene oxide, both classified as probable carcinogens.
  • Petroleum-Derived Ingredients (mineral oil, petrolatum, paraffin): Can be contaminated with PAHs and are persistent in the environment.
  • Drying Alcohols (alcohol denat., isopropyl alcohol): Strip skin's natural oils and compromise the moisture barrier.
  • Synthetic Dyes (FD&C, D&C followed by color and number): Linked to allergic reactions and potential carcinogenic contamination.

How to Choose the Right Clean Body Lotion

By Skin Goal

  • Dry or dehydrated skin: Look for rich emollients like coconut oil, shea butter, and red palm oil. Occlusives help prevent transepidermal water loss, especially in dry climates or winter.
  • Sensitive or reactive skin: Prioritize fragrance‑free formulas with minimal ingredients. Avoid essential oils if you have histamine sensitivity or eczema. The fewer ingredients, the lower the chance of reaction.
  • Oily or combination skin: Choose lightweight, fast-absorbing oils like jojoba, which mimics skin's natural sebum. Avoid heavy butters that can feel greasy or clog pores.
  • Eczema-prone skin: Apply within 3 minutes of bathing when skin is still damp. Seek barrier-supporting ingredients like calendula or oat extracts, and avoid fragrance and essential oils.
  • Families with children: Choose certified organic or EWG Verified products with transparent ingredient lists and fragrance-free formulas safe for all ages.

General Tips

  • Scent sensitivity: Prefer fragrance‑free over "unscented" to avoid masking agents. Learn more in our guide on why unscented skincare products matter.
  • Label clarity: Fewer, familiar ingredients usually mean fewer surprises for reactive skin.
  • After‑shower timing: Apply within 2–3 minutes of bathing to seal in hydration.
  • Third-party certifications: EWG Verified, Made Safe, USDA Organic, or Leaping Bunny provide independent verification beyond marketing claims.

What About Mainstream "Sensitive Skin" Brands?

You've probably seen dermatologist-recommended brands like CeraVe, Aveeno, and Eucerin marketed for sensitive skin. While these brands have made strides toward gentler formulas, it's important to understand what "dermatologist recommended" actually means — and doesn't mean.

The Reality: Dermatologist recommendations often focus on clinical efficacy for specific conditions (like eczema or psoriasis) rather than ingredient purity or organic certification. Many of these products are effective at what they do, but they typically contain synthetic preservatives, petroleum derivatives, and lab-created active ingredients.

  • CeraVe — Uses beneficial ceramides for barrier repair but also contains dimethicone, synthetic polymers, and petrochemicals. Not organic.
  • Aveeno — Contains helpful colloidal oatmeal but most formulas also include synthetic fragrance, dimethicone, and petrolatum.
  • Eucerin — Focuses on clinical hydration but relies heavily on mineral oil and petroleum-derived ingredients.

These products can be appropriate for medical skin conditions under dermatologist guidance, but they don't meet the standard of "clean" or organic that health-conscious consumers seek. If you're looking for truly clean, plant-based care, choose certified organic options instead.

Ingredient Spotlight: What Makes These Ingredients Work

Organic Coconut Oil

Rich in lauric acid, a medium-chain fatty acid with antimicrobial properties. Coconut oil penetrates the skin barrier to soften rough patches and provide deep hydration. When certified organic, it's free from pesticide residues and processed with minimal chemical intervention. For a deeper look at this powerhouse ingredient, see our guide to coconut oil for skin: benefits and everyday uses.

Jojoba Oil

Technically a liquid wax, jojoba closely mimics the molecular structure of human sebum, making it exceptionally compatible with all skin types. It absorbs quickly without greasiness, regulates oil production, and provides antioxidant protection.

Red Palm Oil

One of nature's richest sources of carotenoids (provitamin A) and tocopherols (vitamin E). These antioxidants help protect skin from environmental stressors and support natural regeneration. Its deep orange color indicates high nutrient content.

Shea Butter

Contains high concentrations of fatty acids and vitamins that make it ideal for softening and moisturizing skin. Shea butter forms a protective barrier, has anti-inflammatory properties, and provides extended moisture retention. Unrefined shea butter maintains maximum nutrient content.

How to Use for Best Results

  1. Apply within 2–3 minutes after showering — pat skin 80% dry, then apply lotion to seal in moisture while pores are still open.
  2. Use extra on naturally drier areas — elbows, knees, shins, and heels have fewer oil glands and need extra attention.
  3. Reapply to hands after frequent washing to prevent chapping throughout the day.
  4. Massage thoroughly into skin — 30–60 seconds of circular motions improves absorption and stimulates circulation.
  5. Layer with care — if using serums or oils, apply them first, then seal with body lotion. Wait 1–2 minutes between layers.
  6. Consider seasonal adjustments — richer formulas in winter, lighter ones in humid summer months.
