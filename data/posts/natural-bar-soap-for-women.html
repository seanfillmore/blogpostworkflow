What Actually Makes a Bar Soap 'Natural'?

Here's something the packaging won't tell you: "natural" is not a regulated term in personal care in the United States. The FDA does not define or enforce it. That means any brand can print "natural" on a bar of soap that contains synthetic fragrance, artificial dyes, and petroleum-derived surfactants — and it's completely legal.

So the label isn't the story. The ingredient list is.

Genuinely natural bar soaps are made through a process called saponification — a chemical reaction in which plant-based oils or butters are combined with an alkali (typically sodium hydroxide) to produce soap and glycerin. Cold-process and hot-process are the two most common methods. Cold-process soap is made at lower temperatures, which preserves more of the beneficial properties of the oils. Hot-process uses heat to speed the reaction.

Both methods produce real soap. What they don't produce is the stripping, drying effect you often feel after using a conventional "beauty bar." Here's why: during saponification, glycerin forms naturally as a byproduct. It's a humectant — meaning it draws moisture to the skin. Most large commercial soap manufacturers remove that glycerin and sell it separately for use in lotions and cosmetics. What's left is a bar that cleans but also dries.

Truly natural bar soaps retain that glycerin. That single difference explains a lot of what women notice when they switch — skin that feels soft and comfortable after washing, not tight and squeaky-clean.

The other key distinction: real soap is made from saponified plant oils. Synthetic detergent bars — sometimes called "syndets" — look and feel like soap but are built from lab-made surfactants and preservatives. They're closer to diluted body wash pressed into a bar shape. A natural bar soap should have an ingredient list anchored in recognizable plant oils, not a chemistry textbook.

Ingredients to Avoid in Conventional Soap

Knowing what to skip is just as useful as knowing what to seek. Here are the ingredients most worth watching for on a conventional soap label:

  • Synthetic fragrance (listed as "fragrance" or "parfum"): This single word can legally conceal hundreds of undisclosed chemicals. Many fragrance compounds are known allergens. Some are classified as potential endocrine disruptors — meaning they may interfere with hormonal function. If you have sensitive skin or hormonal skin concerns, this is the first thing to cut.
  • Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) / sodium laureth sulfate (SLES): Harsh synthetic surfactants that produce a lot of foam but strip the skin's natural moisture barrier. SLS in particular is linked to skin irritation and dryness with repeated use. Real Skin Care's bar soap contains neither.
  • Parabens (methylparaben, propylparaben, butylparaben): Synthetic preservatives used to extend shelf life. Research has raised questions about their potential to mimic estrogen in the body. They're common in liquid body washes — less so in real bar soap, but worth checking.
  • Artificial dyes (FD&C colorants): Added purely for appearance. They provide no skin benefit and can cause reactions in sensitive or reactive skin types. A natural bar soap gets any color from the plant oils themselves or from natural clays and botanicals.
  • Triclosan: An antibacterial agent that the FDA restricted from consumer hand soaps in 2016 due to safety concerns. It's largely gone from mainstream soap, but worth knowing as context for why "antibacterial" claims on conventional products deserve scrutiny.

Reading a label with this list in mind takes about 30 seconds. If you see any of the above, put it back.

How to Choose the Right Natural Bar Soap for Your Skin Type

Not every natural bar soap works the same way on every skin type. Here's how to match your skin's needs to the right formulation.

Natural Bar Soap for Dry Skin

Dry skin needs moisture, not more stripping. Look for bars built on coconut oil, olive oil, or shea butter — oils that are structurally similar to skin's natural lipids and help reinforce the moisture barrier rather than break it down. The most important thing a bar soap can offer dry skin is retained glycerin from the saponification process — that natural humectant effect makes a real difference. Avoid bars with a high fragrance load or alcohol-based additives. And if your skin is very dry, pairing a natural bar soap with a clean body lotion is one of the most effective things you can do — see our guide to the best coconut oil body lotions for extremely dry skin for options that work well alongside a natural bar.

Natural Bar Soap for Sensitive Skin

Sensitive skin — including skin affected by eczema, rosacea, or hormonal fluctuations — benefits most from short ingredient lists and no synthetic fragrance. If you react to scented products, look for bars that use a single, mild essential oil at a low concentration, or go fragrance-free entirely. No artificial dyes. No SLS. The fewer ingredients, the easier it is to identify what works for you. Women with reactive skin often do best starting with the simplest bar available and building from there.

Natural Bar Soap for Oily or Acne-Prone Skin

The goal for oily or acne-prone skin isn't to strip oil — it's to cleanse in balance. Bars containing tea tree essential oil are a popular choice here because tea tree may help address surface bacteria and regulate oiliness without over-drying. Lemongrass essential oil can offer similar clarifying benefits. The key is avoiding the "squeaky clean" sensation that signals you've stripped your skin's barrier — which often triggers rebound oil production and makes breakouts worse, not better. Normal or Combination Skin

If your skin is relatively balanced, most quality natural bar soaps will serve you well. Why Bar Soap Is Better Than Body Wash for Many Women

The body wash vs. bar soap debate comes up a lot when women are making the switch to cleaner personal care. Here's the honest answer: for most women who prioritize clean ingredients, bar soap wins on several counts.

First, body wash is water-based — which means it requires preservatives to prevent microbial growth in the bottle. That's one more category of ingredients a bar soap simply doesn't need. Real bar soap is self-preserving because of its low water content and alkaline pH.

Second, bar soap is dramatically better for the environment. Most bars come with minimal or no plastic packaging. A typical natural bar soap also lasts longer per wash than body wash, which tends to be used (and wasted) in larger amounts.

Third, a natural bar soap that retains glycerin from saponification is inherently conditioning in a way that most body washes aren't — even the ones that claim to moisturize. You're getting clean and feeding your skin's moisture barrier at the same time.

How to Use and Store Your Natural Bar Soap for Best Results

Getting the most out of a natural bar soap is mostly about storage. Here's what actually matters:

  • Use a draining soap dish: This is the single biggest factor in how long your bar lasts. A bar that sits in standing water softens and dissolves much faster. A soap dish with drainage slots or a raised rack keeps the bar dry between uses and can easily double its lifespan.
  • Lather in your hands first: Work the bar into a lather in your palms, then apply that lather to your skin. Scrubbing the bar directly against skin is less efficient and uses more soap than you need.
  • Expect a brief adjustment period: If you're switching from a synthetic bar or body wash, your skin may take one to two weeks to recalibrate. Commercial soaps often disrupt the skin's oil production over time. As your skin adjusts to a gentler, glycerin-rich bar, it may briefly feel different before settling into a more balanced state. This is normal.
  • How long should it last? With proper drainage and air-drying between uses, a quality natural bar soap typically lasts four to six weeks with daily full-body use. Some women get longer — it depends on bar size and how carefully it's stored.

Yes — when it's made with quality plant-based oils and retains the natural glycerin produced during saponification. Some natural bars are gentle enough for facial use, particularly those built on a coconut oil or olive oil base. That said, everyone's facial skin is different.
