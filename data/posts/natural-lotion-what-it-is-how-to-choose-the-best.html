What Is a Natural Lotion?

Here's the honest truth: the word "natural" on a lotion label means almost nothing on its own. The FDA does not regulate the use of "natural" for cosmetics. Any brand can slap it on a product — no certification, no ingredient standards, no oversight required.

That's the greenwashing problem in a nutshell. A product can call itself natural while still containing synthetic preservatives, petroleum-derived ingredients, or artificial fragrance. So what should you actually look for?

A genuinely natural body lotion typically checks these boxes:

  • Plant-derived ingredients: Oils, butters, and botanical extracts instead of synthetic or petroleum-based fillers.
  • No parabens or phthalates: These are synthetic preservatives and plasticizers that health-conscious shoppers commonly want to avoid.
  • No synthetic fragrance: Real natural products use essential oils or are fragrance-free — not "parfum."
  • Minimal, recognizable preservatives: Think plant-based options like grapefruit seed extract rather than formaldehyde-releasing chemicals.

Some brands go further and pursue third-party certification — like USDA Organic, EWG Verified, or COSMOS — as external proof that their claims hold up. These certifications aren't mandatory, but they do add a meaningful layer of accountability. If you want to dig deeper into what makes a lotion truly organic, check out our post on organic natural body lotion.

Bottom line: don't trust the front of the label. Read the back.

Key Ingredients to Look For in a Natural Body Lotion

The best natural lotions work because of what's in them — not what's been left out. Here are the hero ingredients worth knowing about, and what each one actually does for your skin.

  • Coconut oil: One of the most studied natural moisturizers out there. Coconut oil is rich in lauric acid, a medium-chain fatty acid that helps strengthen the skin barrier and lock in moisture. It also has natural antimicrobial properties. Our coconut oil body lotion uses both organic virgin coconut oil and organic coconut oil extract as core ingredients — so you're getting the real thing, not a trace amount buried at the bottom of the list.
  • Jojoba oil: Jojoba is technically a wax, not an oil — and that's what makes it special. It closely mimics your skin's natural sebum, which means it absorbs well without clogging pores. Great for both face and body use.
  • Aloe vera: Lightweight and deeply soothing, aloe is a go-to for irritated or sensitive skin. It delivers moisture without heaviness and has natural anti-inflammatory properties.
  • Shea butter: A rich, deeply nourishing emollient that works especially well for dry and sensitive skin. It's packed with fatty acids and vitamin A, which support soft, healthy-looking skin.
  • Cocoa butter: Another heavy-hitter for dry skin. Cocoa butter creates a protective layer on the skin that helps slow moisture loss. It has a natural, subtle scent and a melting texture that feels luxurious.
  • Vitamin E (tocopherol): A fat-soluble antioxidant that helps protect skin cells from oxidative stress and supports repair. Often used alongside plant oils to extend shelf life naturally.
  • Plant-based emulsifying wax: This is what holds the water and oil in a lotion together. A common example is cetearyl alcohol — and yes, it has "alcohol" in the name, but it is nothing like rubbing alcohol. It's a fatty alcohol derived from plants. It's actually moisturizing, not drying. This is one of the more common misconceptions in clean beauty.

Ingredients to Avoid in Conventional Lotions

Most conventional drugstore lotions are inexpensive to produce because they rely on synthetic and petroleum-derived ingredients. That's not always a problem — but for people with sensitive skin or those trying to reduce their chemical exposure, these are worth knowing.

  • Parabens (methylparaben, propylparaben, etc.): Synthetic preservatives used to extend shelf life. They've been studied for potential hormone-disrupting effects, though research is still ongoing. Many shoppers simply prefer to avoid them.
  • Phthalates: Often hidden inside "fragrance" on a label. Phthalates are used to make synthetic scents last longer. They're not required to be listed separately, which is part of why "fragrance" is such a red flag on an ingredient list.
  • Synthetic fragrance / "Parfum": This single word can represent a blend of dozens — sometimes hundreds — of undisclosed chemical compounds. Even if a product smells pleasant, "fragrance" is one of the top triggers for skin sensitivity and allergic reactions.
  • PEGs (polyethylene glycols): These are petroleum-derived compounds used as thickeners and penetration enhancers. They're not inherently dangerous for most people, but they're synthetic and commonly flagged in clean beauty circles.
  • Formaldehyde-releasing preservatives: Ingredients like DMDM hydantoin slowly release small amounts of formaldehyde to prevent bacterial growth. Formaldehyde is a known irritant and classified as a human carcinogen by major health agencies.
  • Mineral oil and petrolatum: Derived from petroleum refining. They do create an occlusive (sealing) layer on skin, but they don't nourish or absorb — they just sit on top. Not harmful for everyone, but not what most natural lotion seekers are after.
  • Synthetic dyes: Added purely for appearance. They offer no skin benefit and can irritate sensitive skin.

The goal here isn't to scare you. Most of these ingredients are approved for cosmetic use and won't hurt the average person. But if you have sensitive skin, are pregnant, or simply want to reduce your daily exposure to synthetics, reading for these on the label is a smart habit.

How to Choose the Best Natural Lotion for Your Skin Type

Not all natural lotions are built the same. A formula that's perfect for dry, flaky skin might feel greasy on combination skin. Here's how to match the right type of all natural body lotion to what your skin actually needs.

Dry Skin

If your skin feels tight, rough, or flaky — especially in winter — you need a lotion with rich, nourishing oils and emollients. Look for formulas built around coconut oil, shea butter, or cocoa butter. These create a moisture barrier and help skin hold onto hydration through the day. For a deeper dive into the best options, our post on best coconut oil body lotions for dry skin is worth a read.

Sensitive Skin

Sensitive skin tends to react to fragrance first — so your best bet is a natural unscented lotion or one scented only with gentle essential oils. Keep the ingredient list short. Aloe, jojoba, and chamomile are all soothing options. Avoid anything with synthetic fragrance, dyes, or alcohol (the drying kind — not the fatty alcohol kind we mentioned earlier).

Normal or Combination Skin

You have more flexibility here. Lightweight oils like jojoba absorb quickly and won't leave a greasy feel. Aloe gel-cream hybrids work well too. The goal is hydration without heaviness — something that softens skin without sitting on top of it.

Face

Your face has different needs than your body. A natural face lotion should be lighter weight and ideally non-comedogenic (meaning it's less likely to clog pores). Jojoba and aloe are both good options for face use. If you have sensitive facial skin specifically, look for a natural face lotion for sensitive skin that avoids both synthetic fragrance and heavy occlusive butters. Always check that a formula is intended for face use before applying it.

General Body Use

For everyday body lotion use, you can go richer. The skin on your body is less reactive than your face and benefits from heavier emollients — especially on areas like elbows, knees, and heels. A coconut oil-based squeeze bottle lotion is a practical, mess-free choice for daily use from head to toe.

Natural Lotion vs. Conventional Lotion: What's the Real Difference?

It's a fair question: is natural lotion actually better, or is it just more expensive? The honest answer is — it depends on the formula. But here's how they typically compare.

Ingredient sourcing: Natural lotions use plant-derived oils, butters, and botanical extracts. Conventional lotions often rely on mineral oil, petrolatum, and synthetic emollients. The plant-derived versions tend to bring additional skin benefits beyond just moisture — like antioxidants, fatty acids, and anti-inflammatory properties.

Preservation: Conventional lotions frequently use parabens or formaldehyde-releasing preservatives. Natural alternatives use ingredients like grapefruit seed extract or vitamin E — both of which do the job without synthetic chemistry. Real Skin Care's formula uses organic grapefruit seed extract as a natural preservative for lotion stability.

Fragrance: Natural lotions use essential oils (like lavender, rose, or geranium) or are fragrance-free. Conventional lotions use synthetic parfum, which can contain phthalates and other undisclosed compounds.

Skin compatibility: For most people with sensitive or reactive skin, natural lotions cause fewer problems — primarily because they cut out synthetic fragrance and harsh preservatives. That said, "natural" doesn't automatically mean safe for everyone. Essential oils can irritate some skin types too. If you're prone to reactions, unscented is always the safer starting point.

The quality of a natural skin lotion comes down to the actual ingredients used — not just the marketing language. A short, recognizable ingredient list with a plant-based emulsifier for lotion and real botanical actives is a better indicator of quality than any label claim.

Frequently Asked Questions About Natural Lotion

Is natural lotion better for sensitive skin?

For most people with sensitive skin, yes. Natural lotions tend to skip the two biggest triggers: synthetic fragrance and harsh preservatives like parabens. That said, essential oils — which many natural lotions use for scent — can still cause reactions in some people. If your skin is especially reactive, start with a natural unscented lotion and patch test before going all-in.

What does "all natural" mean on a lotion label?

Technically, nothing guaranteed. The FDA does not regulate the term "natural" for cosmetics, so any brand can use it freely. When evaluating a product, skip the front label and read the full ingredient list. Genuinely natural formulas use plant-derived ingredients, avoid synthetic preservatives and fragrance, and ideally hold a third-party certification like USDA Organic or EWG Verified.

Can I use natural body lotion on my face?

Sometimes — but not always. Body lotions tend to be richer and heavier than face formulas, which can clog pores on the face (especially if they contain thick oils or butters). If you want to use a natural face lotion, look for a lighter-weight formula specifically formulated for facial skin. Jojoba oil is a good face-friendly ingredient because it's non-comedogenic and absorbs quickly.

How do I know if a lotion is truly natural?

Look past the marketing and read the ingredient list. Red flags include "parfum" or "fragrance," parabens (methylparaben, propylparaben), PEGs, and petroleum-derived ingredients like petrolatum or mineral oil. Green flags include a short list of recognizable plant oils and botanical extracts, essential oils instead of synthetic fragrance, and natural preservatives. Third-party certifications like USDA Organic or EWG Verified add an extra layer of verification.

What is the best natural lotion for dry skin?

For dry skin, you want a formula with rich emollients that create a moisture barrier. Coconut oil, shea butter, and cocoa butter are the heavy hitters. Look for a lotion — not a gel or mist — with a substantial, creamy texture that sinks in rather than evaporating quickly.
