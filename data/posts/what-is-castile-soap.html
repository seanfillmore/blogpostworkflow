What Is Castile Soap? (The Simple Definition)

Castile soap is a vegetable oil-based soap that contains no animal fats and no synthetic detergents. It gets its name from the Castile region of Spain, where olive oil was historically abundant and craftspeople made soap almost entirely from it. That heritage is where the "pure" reputation comes from.

Today, the term "castile soap" has broadened. It now refers to any soap made from 100% plant-derived oils — not just olive oil. Modern versions often blend in coconut oil, hemp seed oil, or other vegetable oils alongside or instead of olive oil. The core identity stays the same: plant oils in, synthetic chemicals out.

What it's not is conventional bar soap. Many standard soaps are made with animal-derived tallow (beef fat) or contain synthetic surfactants like sodium lauryl sulfate, or SLS — a lab-made foaming agent. Castile soap skips all of that. That's what makes it natural castile soap in the truest sense.

What Is Castile Soap Made Of?

At its core, castile soap has just three ingredients: a vegetable oil, an alkali, and water. That's it. Here's what each one does.

  • Vegetable oil base: Traditionally olive oil — smooth, moisturizing, and gentle. Modern castile soaps often use coconut oil (for more lather), hemp seed oil, jojoba oil, or a blend. The oil you start with affects the final bar's hardness, lather quality, and skin feel.
  • An alkali (lye): This is where bar and liquid castile part ways. Bar castile soap uses sodium hydroxide (NaOH), also called lye. Liquid castile soap uses potassium hydroxide (KOH). The difference in the alkali is what determines the final texture — sodium hydroxide makes a harder, solid bar; potassium hydroxide makes a softer, pourable liquid.
  • Water: Used to dissolve the alkali and kick off the chemical reaction.

Now, here's the part people worry about: lye. Many people hear "sodium hydroxide" and get nervous. But here's what you need to know — when oil and lye combine through a reaction called saponification, they transform completely into soap and glycerin. No lye remains in the finished product. The lye is gone. What's left is gentle, skin-safe soap.

Saponification is basically nature's chemistry trick. The fatty acid chains in the oil bind with the alkali and restructure themselves into soap molecules on one end and glycerin on the other. The glycerin is what gives castile soap its naturally moisturizing quality — it's a byproduct of the reaction, and it stays in the bar.

Scented versions of castile soap add essential oils — lavender, peppermint, tea tree, and citrus oils are common. Unscented versions contain only the three core ingredients. Either way, the ingredient list should be short and recognizable. What Is the pH of Castile Soap?

This is one of the most underserved questions in the castile soap conversation — and it actually matters for your skin.

Castile soap is alkaline. Its pH typically falls between 8.9 and 10. Your skin, by contrast, sits at a slightly acidic pH of around 4.5 to 5.5. This difference is called the acid mantle gap, and it's worth understanding.

Your skin's acid mantle is a thin, protective layer that keeps moisture in and bacteria out. When you wash with something highly alkaline, it temporarily disrupts that layer. For most people, the skin rebounds within a few hours. But for those with dry, sensitive, or eczema-prone skin, that disruption can cause tightness, flakiness, or irritation.

This is also why a diluted apple cider vinegar rinse is sometimes recommended after using castile soap as a shampoo — the vinegar (acidic) helps restore the scalp's pH after the alkaline wash. It's a simple fix, but it's good to know going in rather than being surprised by the results.

The pH of castile soap isn't a flaw — it's just context. Knowing it helps you use the product wisely.

What Is Castile Soap Used For? (Everyday Uses)

One of the best things about castile soap is how many jobs it can do. Here's a practical breakdown — with tips on dilution where it counts.

  • Body wash: Dilute before use — a few drops on a wet washcloth or loofah is plenty. Using it straight from a concentrate can feel stripping.
  • Hand soap: This is where castile really shines. Diluted castile works beautifully in a foaming soap dispenser — typically a 1:3 or 1:4 ratio of soap to water. The foaming action stretches it further and makes it gentler. You can also pick up a foam soap refill if you want a ready-made natural hand soap option without mixing your own. Or, if you'd rather skip the DIY altogether, our organic foaming hand soap comes ready to use, already balanced and scented with organic essential oils.
  • Face wash: Best for oily skin types. The alkaline pH can be drying for sensitive or dry skin, so use with caution and follow with a gentle moisturizer if needed.
  • Shampoo: Castile can cleanse the scalp effectively, but be ready for an adjustment period. Always follow with an acidic rinse (diluted apple cider vinegar works) to restore scalp pH and prevent dryness or tangles.
  • Shaving: Creates a workable lather, though it's not as moisturizing as a dedicated shave cream or soap. Fine for occasional use.
  • All-purpose household cleaner: Mix a small amount with water in a spray bottle. Works on countertops, sinks, and floors. Great for castile soap good for cleaning scenarios where you want a plant-based alternative to commercial sprays.
  • Dish soap: Diluted castile can handle lightly greasy dishes by hand. It's not as powerful as commercial dish detergent for heavy grease — keep that expectation in check.
  • Laundry: A small amount works for hand-washing delicates. For machine washing, proceed carefully — too much can create excess suds or leave residue, especially in hard water.
  • Pest control for plants: Diluted castile soap spray is a well-known way to deter soft-bodied insects like aphids and spider mites. A typical ratio is 1–2 teaspoons per quart of water. Spray directly on affected plants, avoiding the hottest part of the day.

Drawbacks and Limitations of Castile Soap

No honest review of castile soap skips this part. "Natural" doesn't automatically mean "right for everyone." Here's what to know before you commit.

  • High pH can irritate sensitive skin: The alkaline nature of castile soap — pH 8.9 to 10 — can disrupt the skin's acid mantle, leading to dryness, tightness, or flare-ups in eczema-prone individuals. This isn't a dealbreaker, but it's real.
  • Hard water incompatibility: Castile soap reacts with the calcium and magnesium minerals in hard water. The result? A white film — sometimes called soap scum — on surfaces, hair, and skin. If your tap water is hard, you may need to rinse more thoroughly or use filtered water.
  • It doesn't disinfect: Castile soap cleans. It does not kill pathogens the way a hospital-grade disinfectant does. If you need true disinfection, you need a product formulated for that.
  • Requires dilution: Concentrated castile soap is not meant to go directly on your skin in most applications. Skipping the dilution step is one of the most common mistakes people make — and it leads to unnecessary irritation.
  • Can strip natural hair oils if overused: Especially without an acidic follow-up rinse. Great for occasional use; not always ideal as a daily shampoo replacement.

Bar Castile Soap vs. Liquid Castile Soap — What's the Difference?

This is a genuinely confusing area, and a lot of sources gloss over it. Let's clear it up.

Both bar and liquid castile soap are made from vegetable oils and an alkali. The difference comes down to which alkali is used. Bar castile soap uses sodium hydroxide (NaOH), which produces a harder, solid bar. Liquid castile soap uses potassium hydroxide (KOH), which produces a softer, pourable product. Same concept, different chemistry, different end result.

From a practical standpoint, liquid castile is easier to dilute and pour into dispensers. Bar castile is more travel-friendly, generates no plastic bottle waste, and is often more economical per wash. Neither is inherently better — it depends on how you want to use it.

One formulation note worth knowing: castile bar soap made primarily from coconut oil will produce a harder bar with more lather than traditional olive oil castile. Coconut oil saponifies differently — it produces more of the fatty acids responsible for bubbles and cleansing power. Who Should (and Shouldn't) Use Castile Soap?

If you're building a cleaner natural skincare routine, castile soap can be a great foundation. But it's not for everyone in every application. Here's a quick self-check.

Castile soap is a good fit if you:

  • Have oily or combination skin that handles alkaline cleansers well
  • Want to avoid synthetic surfactants like SLS or SLES
  • Care about eco-impact — biodegradable, plant-based, low-waste
  • Want one versatile product for body, home, and more
  • Have fragrance sensitivities and opt for the unscented version

Use with caution (or consider an alternative) if you:

  • Have dry, very sensitive, or eczema-prone skin — the alkaline pH may worsen dryness
  • Live in a hard-water area and don't want to deal with soap scum or film on your hair
  • Are expecting a direct shampoo substitute without an adjustment period or acidic rinse step
  • Need a product that disinfects — castile soap cleans but doesn't qualify as a disinfectant

Frequently Asked Questions

Is castile soap the same as regular soap?
No. Castile soap is made from 100% vegetable oils with no animal fats and no synthetic detergents. Many conventional soaps contain tallow (beef fat) or lab-made surfactants like SLS. Castile soap skips all of that — it's pure plant-oil saponification.

Can you use castile soap on your face?
Yes, but it works best for oily skin types. Castile soap has a high pH (8.9–10), which can feel drying or irritating on dry or sensitive skin. If you try it on your face, always dilute it well and follow up with a moisturizer.

What is a good substitute for castile soap?
The closest alternatives are pure glycerin soap, Marseille soap (a French cousin also made from plant oils), or other plant-oil saponified soaps with short, recognizable ingredient lists. The key is a vegetable oil base and no synthetic surfactants.

What aisle is castile soap in?
You'll typically find it in the natural health or body care aisle at grocery stores and health food retailers. It's also widely available online. Liquid versions are often shelved near other liquid soaps or multi-purpose cleaners.

Is castile soap safe for sensitive skin?
When properly diluted, most people tolerate it well. However, its alkaline pH can be irritating for very dry or eczema-prone skin. If you have sensitive skin, dilute generously, patch-test first, and follow up with a moisturizer.

What is the difference between castile soap and dish soap?
Conventional dish soap is formulated with synthetic surfactants designed to cut through heavy grease. Diluted castile soap can handle light hand-washing of dishes, but it's not as powerful against heavy grease or baked-on food. Great for lighter jobs; not a one-to-one swap for commercial dish detergent.
