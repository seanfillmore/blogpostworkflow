What Is Sodium Lauryl Sulfate (SLS)?

Sodium lauryl sulfate — often shortened to SLS — is a synthetic surfactant. In plain language, that means it's a detergent. Its job is to lower the surface tension of water, which creates foam and helps lift particles away from surfaces.

Here's a helpful way to think about it: SLS works a lot like dish soap. Dish soap is great at cutting through grease. But that same aggressive action can be rough on delicate surfaces — and your mouth's soft tissue lining (the mucosa) is about as delicate as it gets.

SLS shows up everywhere in personal care: shampoos, body wash, face wash, and yes, most conventional toothpastes. It's derived from either petroleum or, in some versions, coconut or palm oil — but regardless of its origin, it goes through significant chemical processing before it ends up on your ingredient label.

One thing worth knowing right away: foaming is not the same as cleaning. The bubbles SLS creates feel like deep-cleaning action, but they're largely a sensory illusion. We'll come back to that.

Why Is SLS Added to Toothpaste?

From a manufacturer's perspective, SLS is cheap, effective, and familiar. It makes toothpaste foam, and that foam creates a satisfying sensation that many people associate with a thorough clean. It also helps spread the paste around your mouth more quickly.

But here's the key insight: studies haven't established that SLS meaningfully improves plaque removal compared to SLS-free formulas. The foam is doing a job — but that job is mostly about how the product feels, not how well it actually cleans your teeth.

Real cleaning happens through mechanical brushing and the action of ingredients like baking soda, calcium carbonate, or hydroxyapatite. So if you switch to SLS-free toothpaste and notice it doesn't foam as much, that's not a sign it's working less. It's just more honest about what's happening.

Is Sodium Lauryl Sulfate Safe? What the Research Says

Here's the honest answer: regulatory bodies including the FDA and the EU Cosmetics Regulation consider SLS safe at the concentrations used in rinse-off products like toothpaste. So this isn't a case of something secretly toxic lurking in your bathroom cabinet.

That said, research does connect SLS to some specific issues worth knowing about.

Canker sores: This is the most-studied concern. Multiple clinical trials — including research published in the Journal of Oral Pathology & Medicine — have found that people who use SLS-containing toothpaste experience more frequent and more severe aphthous ulcers (canker sores) than those using SLS-free toothpaste. The connection isn't fully proven as direct cause-and-effect, but the pattern across studies is consistent enough that many dentists recommend SLS-free toothpaste for canker sore-prone patients.

The orange juice effect: Ever brushed your teeth and then had orange juice taste absolutely terrible? SLS is likely the culprit. It degrades phospholipids that normally suppress bitter taste receptors on your tongue. So for an hour or two after brushing, sweet things taste bitter and sour. SLS-free toothpaste largely eliminates this.

Gum and tissue irritation: SLS can disrupt the oral mucosal barrier — the protective lining of your mouth. For sensitive individuals, this leads to irritation, a raw feeling, or even temporary dryness.

For most people, SLS causes no noticeable problems. But for others, switching to SLS-free toothpaste is a genuine improvement — not just a wellness trend.

SLS vs. SLES vs. Natural Surfactant Alternatives

Most SLS guides stop at "SLS is bad, avoid it." But if you're trying to read labels intelligently, you need to know about the other players too.

  • SLS (sodium lauryl sulfate): The harsher of the two common sulfates. It's a smaller molecule, which means it penetrates soft tissue more easily — and that's largely why it's linked to irritation and canker sores.
  • SLES (sodium laureth sulfate): The ethoxylated version. It's been chemically modified to be slightly milder. But it's still synthetic, and the ethoxylation process can leave trace amounts of 1,4-dioxane — a potential contaminant that's been flagged by consumer safety groups. Milder than SLS, but not a clean ingredient.
  • Sodium cocoyl glutamate: A naturally-derived surfactant made from coconut oil and fermented sugars. Gentle, biodegradable, and well-tolerated by sensitive skin and oral tissues. You'll find this in truly clean personal care products.
  • Decyl glucoside: Derived from coconut oil and glucose. Very mild, non-irritating, and also biodegradable. Common in gentle formulas for kids and sensitive users.
  • Coco-betaine: Another coconut-derived surfactant. Gentler than SLS or SLES and often used in sensitive-skin shampoos and cleansers.

Some natural toothpastes skip surfactants altogether — using coconut oil as the base instead. Coconut oil doesn't foam, but it does help carry ingredients across the teeth and gums effectively. Real Skin Care's formula takes this approach, which means there's no synthetic surfactant of any kind.

Who Should Definitely Switch to SLS-Free Toothpaste?

SLS-free toothpaste is a good choice for almost anyone. But for some people, it's less of a preference and more of a genuine need.

  • Canker sore sufferers: The clinical evidence here is the strongest. If you get recurring mouth ulcers, eliminating SLS is one of the most straightforward steps you can try. Many people notice a meaningful reduction in frequency after switching.
  • People with sensitive gums or oral mucosa: If your gums bleed easily, feel raw after brushing, or you've been told you have thin or sensitive tissue, SLS-free toothpaste reduces daily chemical irritation.
  • Those with dry mouth (xerostomia): SLS can make dry mouth worse by further disrupting the mucosal barrier. If you already struggle with saliva production, you don't need an added irritant in your toothpaste.
  • Kids and toddlers: Children's oral tissues are still developing and tend to be more sensitive than adults'. Many parents look for kids' toothpaste without sodium lauryl sulfate — especially for toddlers who are still likely to swallow some paste. Gentler formulas are the smart call.
  • People with sulfate sensitivities: If you've already switched to sulfate-free shampoo or body wash because SLS irritates your skin or scalp, the same logic applies in your mouth.

Once you've decided SLS-free is right for you, the next question is whether fluoride matters for your routine — and that's worth addressing directly.

Fluoride or Fluoride-Free? What to Know When Going SLS-Free

Many people searching for toothpaste without sodium lauryl sulfate are also thinking about going fluoride-free at the same time. These are actually two separate decisions — but they often come up together.

Fluoride has strong, long-established evidence behind its cavity-prevention effects. Most conventional dentists recommend it. At the same time, some consumers prefer to avoid fluoride due to concerns about dental fluorosis (white spots on teeth from excess fluoride in children), potential effects at very high systemic doses, or simply a preference for fully natural products.

The good news is that you have real options either way:

  • Fluoride toothpaste without SLS: For those who want the cavity protection of fluoride but want to cut out the harsh surfactant. Several brands now offer this combination.
  • Toothpaste without fluoride and sodium lauryl sulfate: For those going fully clean. This is where natural, ingredient-minimal formulas come in. Real Skin Care's fluoride-free toothpaste and non-fluoride toothpaste collections cover this space.

If your dentist has specifically recommended fluoride for cavity prevention or enamel repair, don't abandon it without talking to them first. But if you're a generally healthy adult with good dental habits, a clean fluoride-free formula can absolutely be part of a solid oral care routine.

How to Read a Toothpaste Label: What to Look For and Avoid

Label reading gets easier once you know what you're looking for. Here's a quick reference you can use the next time you're standing in the toothpaste aisle.

Red flags — ingredients to avoid:

  • Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS): The main surfactant to avoid.
  • Sodium laureth sulfate (SLES): Slightly milder but still synthetic; may contain trace contaminants.
  • Ammonium lauryl sulfate: Another sulfate surfactant in the same family.
  • Artificial dyes: FD&C Blue No. 1, FD&C Red No. 40 — no benefit to your teeth, aesthetic only.
  • Saccharin: A synthetic sweetener linked to digestive sensitivity in some people.
  • Propylene glycol: A synthetic humectant that some sensitive individuals prefer to avoid.

Green flags — ingredients that signal a cleaner formula:

  • Coconut oil (or organic virgin coconut oil): A natural, non-foaming base with well-documented antimicrobial properties in laboratory studies.
  • Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate): Gentle abrasive that helps polish teeth and neutralize acids.
  • Essential oils (peppermint, spearmint, cinnamon, clove): Natural flavoring with antimicrobial activity in laboratory settings.
  • Stevia: A plant-derived sweetener with no known negative effects on teeth.
  • Myrrh: A traditional botanical used for centuries in oral care, with emerging research on its antimicrobial properties.
  • Xanthan gum: A plant-derived thickener — no concern here.
  • Sodium cocoyl glutamate / decyl glucoside: If a formula does use a surfactant, these naturally-derived options are far gentler than SLS.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does SLS-free toothpaste clean as well as regular toothpaste?
Yes. Actual cleaning comes from the mechanical action of your toothbrush and active ingredients like baking soda — not from foam. SLS creates a foaming sensation that feels like deep cleaning, but studies haven't shown it improves plaque removal. You can get a thoroughly clean mouth without it.

How do I know if a toothpaste is truly SLS-free?
Read the full ingredient list — not the front label. Look for "sodium lauryl sulfate" or "sodium laureth sulfate" in the inactive ingredients section. If neither appears, the formula is SLS-free. Certified organic toothpastes are inherently SLS-free, since SLS is a synthetic detergent that doesn't qualify under organic standards.

Does SLS-free toothpaste work for sensitive teeth?
Yes — and it's often the recommended starting point for anyone with sensitivity. SLS can irritate the oral mucosa and temporarily worsen tooth sensitivity in susceptible individuals. Removing it eliminates a common irritant without compromising any of the cleaning or remineralization benefits that make toothpaste effective.

Is SLS-free toothpaste safe for kids?
Yes — and it's often the better choice for children. Kids' oral tissues are more sensitive, and children are more likely to swallow some toothpaste, especially when young. Toothpaste without sodium lauryl sulfate for kids removes one source of potential irritation. A fluoride-free AND SLS-free formula gives parents the peace of mind that comes with truly simple ingredients.

Can switching to SLS-free toothpaste reduce canker sores?
Clinical research suggests it may help for some people. Several randomized controlled trials have found that participants using SLS-free toothpaste experienced fewer and less severe canker sores compared to those using SLS-containing formulas. It's not a guaranteed cure, but it's one of the most evidence-supported lifestyle changes for canker sore-prone individuals.

What is the difference between SLS-free and fluoride-free toothpaste?
These are completely separate attributes. SLS refers to the foaming agent; fluoride refers to a cavity-prevention mineral. A toothpaste can be SLS-free but still contain fluoride. It can be fluoride-free but still contain SLS. Or it can be both — like Real Skin Care's formula. When you're shopping, check for both ingredients separately on the label.
