What Is SLS (Sodium Lauryl Sulfate) in Toothpaste?

SLS stands for sodium lauryl sulfate. It's a synthetic surfactant — basically a foaming agent — that's added to toothpaste to make it lather up when you brush. You've probably seen it in shampoos, body washes, and dish soap too. It's very common.

Here's the thing: SLS doesn't actually clean your teeth. The foam it creates is mostly a sensory experience. Actual cleaning comes from the mechanical action of brushing, mild abrasives in the formula, and active ingredients like fluoride. The lather from SLS is there because we've come to expect it — it signals "this is working" even though it isn't really doing the cleaning work.

SLS is derived from lauric acid (which occurs naturally in coconut oil and palm kernel oil), but the finished ingredient is highly processed and chemically distinct from those natural sources. On a toothpaste label regulated by the FDA, you'll see it listed by its full name: Sodium Lauryl Sulfate, usually in the inactive ingredients section.

Research published in the Journal of Oral Pathology & Medicine has examined SLS's interaction with the oral mucosa — the soft tissue lining your mouth — and found it can be irritating for some people, particularly with repeated daily exposure. That research is part of why SLS-free formulas have grown in popularity well beyond the natural-beauty niche.

Why Would You Want Toothpaste Without SLS?

There are several good reasons to seek out SLS-free toothpaste. Not all of them apply to everyone — and SLS isn't harmful for most people. But if you fall into one or more of these categories, it's worth considering the switch.

  • Canker sores and mouth ulcers: This is the biggest one. Multiple studies have found a link between SLS in toothpaste and the frequency and severity of aphthous ulcers (the clinical term for canker sores). One frequently cited study in the Journal of Oral Pathology & Medicine found that participants using SLS-free toothpaste had noticeably fewer canker sores than those using a standard SLS-containing formula. If you get recurring mouth sores, switching to toothpaste without SLS for canker sores is one of the first things many dentists suggest trying.
  • Gum and mouth sensitivity: SLS can disrupt the mucosal lining in your mouth. For people whose gums are already irritated or who have sensitive oral tissue, that repeated irritation adds up.
  • Taste disruption — the orange juice effect: Ever noticed that orange juice tastes awful right after brushing? That's partly SLS. It temporarily suppresses sweetness receptors while enhancing bitter ones, which is why your morning juice tastes sour and metallic. SLS-free toothpaste tends not to cause this.
  • Clean beauty habits: Many people who already avoid SLS in their shampoo or face wash extend that preference to their toothpaste. It makes sense — consistency in ingredient philosophy matters if you're intentional about what goes on (and in) your body.
  • SLS sensitivity from skincare: If SLS irritates your skin in other products, there's good reason to keep it away from the delicate tissue in your mouth too.

To be clear: SLS is not proven dangerous for most people. The science doesn't support calling it "toxic." But for sensitive individuals, it's a legitimate irritant — and since it doesn't contribute to cleaning, there's no real downside to leaving it out.

Does SLS-Free Toothpaste Still Clean Your Teeth?

Yes. Absolutely. And this is probably the most important thing to understand before making the switch.

Foam does not equal clean. The cleaning work in toothpaste comes from three things: the physical act of brushing, mild abrasives that polish the tooth surface (like baking soda or hydrated silica), and active ingredients like fluoride that help protect against cavities. SLS contributes none of that. It just makes the paste feel frothy.

When you use a toothpaste without sodium lauryl sulfate, you might notice it doesn't foam as much. That can feel strange at first. But your teeth will get just as clean — and for many people, their mouths feel better afterward.

It's worth noting that many dentists specifically recommend SLS-free formulas for patients with recurring canker sores, gum sensitivity, or other oral tissue concerns. The foam is cosmetic. The cleaning is real either way.

How to Read a Toothpaste Label to Spot SLS

Reading toothpaste labels can feel a little confusing because the FDA requires toothpaste to list "active" and "inactive" ingredients separately. SLS is always an inactive ingredient — you'll find it listed as Sodium Lauryl Sulfate in that section. If you don't see it there, the formula is SLS-free.

There's a close cousin to watch for too: SLES, or Sodium Laureth Sulfate. It's a similar surfactant that some brands use instead. It's generally considered slightly gentler than SLS, but clean-beauty consumers often prefer to avoid both. Cocamidopropyl betaine is another surfactant that sometimes appears in toothpaste — it's generally gentler than SLS and less likely to cause irritation.

Some readers also look for toothpaste without SLS and triclosan or toothpaste without SLS and titanium dioxide. Triclosan was largely phased out of toothpaste after the FDA restricted its use in over-the-counter antiseptic products. Titanium dioxide (used as a whitening colorant) is another ingredient some clean-beauty consumers prefer to avoid. If those matter to you, scan the inactive ingredients list for all three. And since formulas change, always verify the current label — even if a product was SLS-free last year, it's worth confirming today.
