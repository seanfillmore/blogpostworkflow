Why People Use Coconut Oil for Brushing Teeth

Interest in coconut oil for oral hygiene keeps growing, and for good reason. Coconut oil is rich in lauric acid, a medium-chain fatty acid that makes up about 45-50% of coconut oil's composition. Lauric acid has powerful antibacterial properties that specifically target harmful bacteria in your mouth.

Many people choose coconut oil to avoid common toothpaste ingredients like fluoride, SLS (sodium lauryl sulfate), artificial sweeteners, and synthetic preservatives. For those with sensitive mouths, allergies to commercial ingredients, or who simply prefer minimalist routines, coconut oil offers a natural alternative.

The 2026 wellness trend has especially embraced oil pulling and DIY oral care, with thousands sharing their experiences on social media. But beyond the trends, what does research actually show?

Benefits of Using Coconut Oil on Your Teeth

Research on coconut oil's oral health benefits has grown significantly. Here's what we know:

Reduces Harmful Bacteria

A 2025 clinical trial published in Clinical Oral Investigations found that coconut oil with high lauric acid content (47.92%) significantly reduced harmful oral bacteria. Lauric acid specifically fights Streptococcus mutans, one of the main bacteria responsible for tooth decay and cavities. The study showed coconut oil had comparable antibacterial effects to chlorhexidine (a common prescription mouthwash) but with fewer side effects.

Fights Plaque and Gum Inflammation

Multiple studies have shown coconut oil is effective at reducing plaque buildup and improving gum health. The same 2025 research found that coconut oil may provide additional anti-inflammatory benefits beyond just killing bacteria, likely due to lauric acid's direct action on inflammatory compounds in gum tissue.

Natural Whitening

Coconut oil may help lift surface stains on teeth without harsh bleaching agents. While you won't get dramatic whitening like professional treatments, consistent use can help remove stains from coffee, tea, and other foods by reducing plaque that traps stain particles.

Freshens Breath

Because bad breath is often caused by bacteria, coconut oil's antibacterial effects can naturally neutralize odor-causing bacteria. Many users report fresher breath after switching to coconut oil toothpaste or oil pulling.

Gentle on Sensitive Mouths

Unlike toothpastes with SLS (which causes canker sores in many people), coconut oil is gentle and soothing. It doesn't cause tissue irritation, burning, or peeling that some people experience with commercial toothpastes.

No Harsh Chemicals

Pure coconut oil is completely free from preservatives, foaming agents, synthetic dyes, artificial sweeteners, and other additives found in conventional toothpastes.

Coconut Oil Pulling vs Brushing with Coconut Oil

There are two main ways people use coconut oil for oral health, and they're quite different:

Oil Pulling

Oil pulling is an ancient Ayurvedic practice where you swish a tablespoon of coconut oil in your mouth for 15-20 minutes. The extended time allows the oil to "pull" bacteria and toxins from your mouth. Research shows oil pulling requires this longer duration to maximize its cleaning and antibacterial effects.

Oil pulling is best done first thing in the morning on an empty stomach. The process takes time but may offer deeper cleaning benefits. Spit the oil into a trash can (never your sink, as it can clog pipes) and rinse with warm water afterward.

Brushing with Coconut Oil

Brushing with coconut oil means applying it directly to your toothbrush and brushing for 2 minutes like you would with regular toothpaste. It's quicker and more convenient than oil pulling, though you may not get the same deep-cleaning benefits.

You can do both in one routine: oil pull in the morning, then brush with coconut oil or regular toothpaste afterward for the best results.

How to Brush Your Teeth with Coconut Oil

If you want to try brushing with pure coconut oil, here's how:

  1. Scoop ½ to 1 teaspoon of virgin coconut oil onto your toothbrush. If the oil is solid (it solidifies below 76°F), let it soften slightly on the brush or warm it between your fingers.
  2. Brush gently for 2 minutes, covering all tooth surfaces just like with regular toothpaste. The oil will turn liquid quickly in your mouth.
  3. Spit into a trash can, not your sink. Oil can solidify in pipes and cause clogs over time.
  4. Rinse with warm water or follow with a natural mouthwash if desired.

Pro Tip: For added cleaning power, you can mix coconut oil with a small amount of baking soda and a few drops of peppermint oil. This creates a more abrasive paste that helps remove surface stains while adding fresh flavor.

DIY Coconut Oil Toothpaste Recipes

Making your own coconut oil toothpaste is easier than you think. Here are two popular recipes that cost a fraction of store-bought natural toothpastes:

Basic 3-Ingredient Coconut Oil Toothpaste

Ingredients:

  • 6 tablespoons organic virgin coconut oil
  • 4 tablespoons baking soda
  • 15-20 drops food-grade peppermint oil (or spearmint, cinnamon)
  • Pinch of sea salt (optional)

Instructions:

  1. Leave the coconut oil in a warm spot to soften (don't melt it completely).
  2. Mix the baking soda and salt together in a small bowl.
  3. Add the softened coconut oil and stir until you get a smooth, thick paste.
  4. Mix in the peppermint oil for flavor.
  5. Transfer to a small glass jar with a lid. Store at room temperature away from sunlight.
  6. Use within 2-3 weeks.

To use: Scoop a small amount onto your toothbrush with a clean spoon (don't dip your brush directly into the jar to avoid bacteria). Brush for 2 minutes, then spit and rinse.

Whitening Coconut Oil Toothpaste with Calcium

This more advanced recipe includes remineralizing ingredients:

Ingredients:

  • 6 tablespoons organic virgin coconut oil
  • 3 tablespoons baking soda
  • 2 tablespoons calcium carbonate powder (or food-grade bentonite clay)
  • 1 tablespoon xylitol powder (natural sweetener that fights cavities)
  • 20 drops peppermint oil
  • 5 drops tea tree oil (optional, for extra antibacterial power)

Instructions:

  1. Follow the same mixing process as the basic recipe.
  2. The calcium carbonate helps remineralize enamel while you brush.
  3. Xylitol adds sweetness and has been shown to reduce cavity-causing bacteria.
  4. Store in a glass jar for up to 3 weeks.

Storage and Safety Tips

  • Always use a clean spoon to scoop toothpaste to avoid contamination
  • Coconut oil may become runny in warm temperatures (above 76°F) or solid in cold
  • Never spit coconut oil down the drain—always use a trash can
  • Start slowly if you have sensitive teeth, as baking soda can be abrasive with heavy pressure
  • Use food-grade essential oils only, not fragrance oils

Is Coconut Oil as Effective as Regular Toothpaste?

This is the important question. While coconut oil has real benefits, it's not a complete replacement for toothpaste in most cases.

Here's why: Coconut oil is excellent at reducing bacteria and plaque, but it does not contain ingredients that strengthen or remineralize tooth enamel. Enamel is the hard outer layer of your teeth that protects against cavities. Once it's damaged, it can't grow back on its own.

Fluoride toothpaste is proven to strengthen enamel and prevent cavities. For people who want to avoid fluoride, alternatives like nano-hydroxyapatite (a mineral that mimics natural tooth enamel) can help remineralize teeth. Coconut oil alone doesn't do this.

Bottom line: Most dentists recommend using coconut oil as a complement to toothpaste, not a complete replacement—especially if you're cavity-prone, have thin enamel, or drink acidic beverages that wear down enamel over time.

Who Should Try Coconut Oil for Oral Care?

Coconut oil toothpaste or brushing works best for:

  • People with sensitive mouths who react badly to SLS or other toothpaste ingredients
  • Those avoiding chemicals and preferring minimalist, natural routines
  • Adults with healthy teeth looking to supplement their oral care
  • Anyone interested in oil pulling as part of their wellness routine
  • People with canker sore problems (SLS is a major trigger)

However, coconut oil alone is not recommended for:

  • Children under 6 (consult pediatric dentist first)
  • Adults at high risk for cavities
  • People with weak enamel or active decay
  • Anyone whose dentist recommends fluoride treatment

The best approach for most people is to use both: coconut oil for its antibacterial benefits, plus a remineralizing toothpaste (fluoride or hydroxyapatite) for cavity protection.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I brush my teeth with coconut oil only?

You can brush with coconut oil alone, but most dentists recommend using it alongside a toothpaste that provides cavity protection. Coconut oil fights bacteria but doesn't strengthen enamel like fluoride or remineralize like hydroxyapatite. If you have good oral health and aren't cavity-prone, you might be fine with just coconut oil—but check with your dentist first.

How often should I use coconut oil for oral care?

Once daily is effective for most people. You can use it as toothpaste or for oil pulling in the morning. Some people alternate between coconut oil and regular toothpaste for balance—for example, coconut oil in the morning and fluoride-free toothpaste at night.

Does coconut oil prevent cavities?

Coconut oil reduces harmful bacteria that cause cavities, which lowers your risk. However, it doesn't remineralize enamel the way fluoride or hydroxyapatite does. Think of it as helping prevent the bacteria that cause cavities, but not rebuilding or strengthening enamel once damage starts. It works best as part of a complete oral care routine.

Is coconut oil safe for kids?

Consult your pediatric dentist before using coconut oil with children, especially under age 6. Most dental professionals recommend fluoride toothpaste for young children to prevent cavities during critical tooth development years. Coconut oil alone may not provide enough protection for kids who are still learning proper brushing habits or who consume sugary foods.

What type of coconut oil is best for teeth?

Use organic, unrefined virgin coconut oil for the highest lauric acid content and purest results. Look for oils labeled with at least 45-50% lauric acid content. Refined coconut oil loses some beneficial properties during processing. The 2025 study showing oral health benefits used virgin coconut oil with 47.92% lauric acid content.

Can coconut oil whiten teeth?

Coconut oil may help lift surface stains over time by reducing plaque buildup that traps stain particles. Results are gradual—expect subtle brightening rather than dramatic whitening. It works best when combined with baking soda in a DIY recipe. Don't expect bleaching-level results, but you may see improvement in overall tooth brightness with consistent use over several weeks.

What's the difference between oil pulling and brushing with coconut oil?

Oil pulling means swishing a tablespoon of coconut oil in your mouth for 15-20 minutes to pull toxins and bacteria. This Ayurvedic practice requires time and patience but may offer deeper cleaning. Brushing with coconut oil is quicker—just 2 minutes like regular brushing—but may not provide the same deep antibacterial benefits. You can do both: oil pull in the morning, then brush afterward.

Why shouldn't I spit coconut oil into the sink?

Coconut oil solidifies at room temperature (below 76°F) and can clog your pipes over time, leading to expensive plumbing problems. The oil can accumulate in pipes and create blockages. Always spit coconut oil into a trash can, compost bin, or disposable cup. Wait a few minutes after rinsing before running water down the drain.
