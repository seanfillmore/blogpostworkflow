Most people brush their teeth twice a day, every single day, for their entire lives. And almost no one reads the ingredients label on their toothpaste. That's totally understandable — it's toothpaste, not a prescription drug. But there's one ingredient showing up in the majority of conventional toothpastes that a growing number of consumers, and some dentists, are starting to question. It's called sodium lauryl sulfate, and it's the main reason your toothpaste foams.

SLS isn't a secret ingredient. It's not banned anywhere. For most people, it's probably fine most of the time. But "probably fine most of the time" is a lower bar than you might want for something you put in your mouth every single day. This post covers what SLS actually is, why brands use it, what the real documented concerns are, who should genuinely consider switching, and what a clean SLS-free toothpaste actually looks like.

What Is SLS in Toothpaste?

Sodium lauryl sulfate — or SLS — is a surfactant. A surfactant is an ingredient that lowers the surface tension of a liquid, which helps it spread and lift away oils, debris, and bacteria. Think of it like dish soap for your mouth. Chemically, SLS is an anionic surfactant derived from lauryl alcohol, which can come from coconut oil, palm oil, or petroleum sources depending on the manufacturer.

In toothpaste, SLS does two main things: it creates foam, and it helps distribute other ingredients evenly across your teeth and gums. You'll also find it in shampoos, body washes, and face cleansers for the same reason — it's a well-established, inexpensive foaming agent that has been used in personal care for decades.

It can appear on ingredient labels under more than one name. Here's what to look for:

How to spot SLS on a toothpaste label: Look for sodium lauryl sulfate, sodium dodecyl sulfate, or the abbreviation SLS or SDS in the ingredient list. If any of those appear, the toothpaste contains SLS.

It's worth noting that SLS is different from SLES (sodium laureth sulfate), which is a related but milder compound. Some brands swap SLS for SLES, but that's not the same as going SLS-free. Always check the full ingredient list rather than relying on front-of-pack claims.

Why Do Toothpaste Brands Put SLS in Their Formulas?

There are real, practical reasons SLS became a staple in toothpaste formulas — and it's not a conspiracy. Here's what's driving it.

First, foam sells. Consumers associate a thick lather with cleaning power. It feels like something is happening. SLS creates that sensation efficiently and cheaply. Second, SLS helps active ingredients like fluoride or baking soda spread more evenly across tooth surfaces and reach into the spaces between teeth. That's a legitimate functional benefit. Third, it's cheap and shelf-stable, which matters at scale.

Here's the thing, though: foam has nothing to do with how well your toothpaste cleans. That part is handled by abrasives and active ingredients. The foam is largely a sensory experience — one that we've been conditioned to expect. SLS-free toothpastes foam less, and some don't foam at all. That can feel strange at first. But it doesn't mean they're cleaning less effectively.

Most mainstream toothpaste lines — including standard Crest, Colgate, and Oral-B products — still use SLS. Some brands, like Sensodyne Pronamel, have moved away from it in certain formulations, largely in response to the sensitivity and irritation concerns we'll get into next.

Is SLS in Toothpaste Bad for You? The Honest Answer

Let's be direct: SLS in toothpaste is not acutely dangerous for most people. It's FDA-approved for use in oral care products at regulated concentrations (typically around 1–2% in toothpaste). You're not swallowing it — you're rinsing it out. Regulatory bodies in the US and EU have reviewed SLS and found no credible evidence that it causes cancer or systemic harm at these levels. Anyone telling you otherwise is overstating the science.

That said, "not acutely dangerous" is not the same as "universally harmless." The documented concerns with SLS in oral care are real — they're just more mundane than the scary headlines suggest.

SLS is a known irritant. At the concentrations used in toothpaste, SLS can disrupt the mucosal lining of the mouth. That lining — made up of proteins including mucin — serves as a protective barrier for the soft tissue inside your cheeks, lips, and gums. Daily exposure to SLS can gradually degrade that barrier, leaving tissue more vulnerable to irritation.

The dry mouth connection is real. SLS disrupts the mucosal film that helps retain saliva and keep oral tissue lubricated. For people who already experience dry mouth (xerostomia), this can make things noticeably worse. Dry mouth isn't just uncomfortable — it raises the risk of cavities, because saliva is a key defense against bacteria.

Perioral and skin reactions occur in some people. SLS residue left around the lips or chin can trigger perioral dermatitis — a rash-like skin condition — or worsen existing eczema. Some people also experience allergic contact reactions to SLS, which can show up as redness, swelling, or peeling skin around the mouth.

The bottom line: SLS is not acutely dangerous for most people, but it is an unnecessary irritant — especially for sensitive individuals — and many dentists now recommend SLS-free formulas as a default, particularly for patients with recurrent oral irritation or chronic dry mouth.

Can SLS Toothpaste Cause Canker Sores?

This is one of the most searched questions about SLS, and the evidence here is more specific than for some of the other concerns. Canker sores (recurrent aphthous ulcers) are small, painful ulcers that form inside the mouth. They have multiple potential triggers — stress, certain foods, hormonal shifts, nutritional deficiencies. But SLS has been specifically investigated as one of them.

The most frequently cited study is a 1994 paper by Herlofson and Barkvoll, published in the Journal of Clinical Periodontology. The researchers found that patients using SLS-containing toothpaste experienced significantly more canker sores than those using an SLS-free formula. The proposed mechanism makes chemical sense: SLS strips the protective mucin coating from the oral mucosa, leaving the tissue more exposed and more vulnerable to abrasion and ulceration. Later research has broadly supported this direction, though scientists acknowledge that canker sores are multifactorial and SLS is unlikely to be the sole cause for everyone.

If you get canker sores regularly, switching to an SLS-free toothpaste is one of the simplest, lowest-risk interventions you can try. Give it four to six weeks. Some people report a meaningful reduction in frequency. Others don't notice a change, which may mean their canker sores have a different primary trigger. Either way, you lose nothing by trying.

We want to be clear: making the switch to SLS-free toothpaste is not a cure for canker sores. But the evidence is strong enough that it's worth testing — especially since SLS serves no therapeutic function in your toothpaste to begin with.

Who Should Definitely Switch to SLS-Free Toothpaste?

For some people, ditching SLS is genuinely worth prioritizing. Here's who stands to benefit most:

  • People with recurrent canker sores or mouth ulcers: As described above, SLS-free toothpaste for canker sores is one of the first things worth trying if you get them regularly.
  • Anyone with sensitive teeth or gum sensitivity: SLS can exacerbate gum irritation. Sensitive toothpaste without SLS is a gentler option for anyone whose mouth feels raw or tender after brushing.
  • People with dry mouth (xerostomia): SLS degrades the mucosal film that helps retain saliva. If dry mouth is already an issue, daily SLS exposure makes it harder for your body to manage. SLS-free toothpaste for dry mouth is a simple swap.
  • Those with perioral dermatitis or lip irritation: SLS residue around the lip line is a known trigger for perioral dermatitis — a persistent rash that develops around the mouth. If you've struggled with unexplained chin or jawline skin irritation, your toothpaste could be a contributing factor. SLS-free formulas remove that variable.
  • People with a known SLS allergy or contact sensitivity: SLS allergy symptoms can include redness, swelling, peeling, or a burning sensation in or around the mouth. If that sounds familiar, this is a non-negotiable switch.
  • Children: Kids have smaller mouths, thinner mucosal tissue, and often swallow some toothpaste — especially younger children. Many pediatric dentists now recommend SLS-free toothpaste for kids as a precaution.
  • People following a natural personal care lifestyle: If you're already moving toward a natural toothpaste and minimizing synthetic surfactant exposure across your routine, switching away from SLS is consistent with that approach.

That said, you don't need to be in any of those groups to make the switch. There's genuinely no downside to using SLS-free toothpaste. It cleans just as well (more on that below), and you're removing an irritant with a known track record. For many people, it's simply a low-effort upgrade.

What to Look for in an SLS-Free Toothpaste

Going SLS-free is a good start. But what else should you be looking at when you pick up a new toothpaste?

  • What replaces SLS as a surfactant: Some brands use gentler surfactant alternatives like sodium cocoyl glutamate or decyl glucoside, which are milder on mucosal tissue. Others — like our own coconut oil toothpaste — use organic coconut oil as the base. Coconut oil has a long history of use in oral care, and it contributes to the paste's texture and spread without a harsh surfactant.
  • Fluoride vs. fluoride-free — your call: Going SLS-free doesn't mean you have to give up fluoride. Plenty of SLS-free toothpastes include fluoride; plenty don't. This is a separate personal and dental decision. If you prefer fluoride-free, you can explore options like our fluoride-free toothpaste collection. If your dentist recommends fluoride, you can still find SLS-free fluoride toothpastes — they're not mutually exclusive.
  • Other ingredients worth skipping: While you're reading the label, look out for artificial sweeteners like saccharin, artificial dyes, and carrageenan (a thickening agent linked to digestive irritation in some people). A clean SLS-free formula should be short and readable.
  • Natural actives that genuinely contribute: Ingredients like baking soda have a well-documented role in oral care — they're mildly abrasive, help neutralize acids, and support a healthy mouth environment. Clove, cinnamon, and myrrh have histories of use in traditional oral care and are studied for their antimicrobial properties in laboratory research. When you see these in an SLS-free formula, they're earning their place.

Is SLS-Free Toothpaste as Effective as Regular Toothpaste?

Yes. This is the most common hesitation, and it's worth addressing head-on.

SLS has no direct antibacterial or anti-cavity function. Its role in toothpaste is purely mechanical — it creates foam and helps the paste spread. Studies have not found that removing SLS reduces plaque removal efficacy when other effective ingredients like baking soda or fluoride are present. The cleaning work is done by the actives and abrasives in the formula, not the foam.

The less-foam experience can feel unfamiliar for the first week or two. That's conditioning, not chemistry. Once you adjust, most people find the clean, low-foam brush feels normal — and often more comfortable, particularly if they'd been experiencing any of the irritation issues described above.

Some dentists actually prefer recommending SLS-free toothpaste for patients recovering from oral procedures, managing chronic gum sensitivity, or dealing with recurring mouth sores — precisely because SLS offers no clinical benefit while carrying a real irritation risk for those patients.

Bottom line: switching to a toothpaste without SLS is not a trade-off. You're removing an unnecessary ingredient, not a functional one.
